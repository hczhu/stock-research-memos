- ## Main Narrative
	- > What will the business look like in 5–10 years? Is the market big enough for 10x? Can it 4x revenue in 10 years?
	- **The one-line story**: *Atlassian is the operating system for how teams work — it will follow ServiceNow's trajectory from a developer tool to a \$10–12B revenue enterprise platform, compounding through product releases, enterprise up-market motion, and AI-native features.*
	- **ServiceNow analog**: ServiceNow grew 57× to \$13B over 14 years post-IPO (36% CAGR) as essentially a one-trick pony facing Microsoft competition. Atlassian has more products (Jira, Confluence, JSM, Loom, Rovo) and a larger but lower-ARPU customer base. Management target: \$10B+ revenue by FY2029 at 20%+ growth — roughly **1.7× the current \$5.9B LTM of 2026Q1 revenue**.
	- **The platform flywheel**: 350,000 customers × 8,000+ marketplace apps × 1.2M installs × 1,800+ partners = an ecosystem you can vibe-code around but cannot replicate. Atlassian is the workflow substrate of modern knowledge work.
	- **The AI layer (Rovo)**: Rovo is Atlassian's AI layer — enterprise search + agents running on the **Teamwork Graph**, which models people, work relationships, and knowledge across Jira, Confluence, and 100+ third-party connectors. Unlike generic LLMs, Rovo understands org-specific context. The Service Collection (JSM + CSM + Assets + Rovo) already crossed \$1B ARR and is growing >30% YoY.
	- **Two historical re-accelerations** as a template: 2018 (Trello acquisition + JSM hitting critical mass + price increases) and 2022 (forced Server → Cloud migration that repriced legacy users to recurring subscriptions). The next catalyst is the Data Center end-of-life (2029) + Rovo AI monetization + enterprise up-market motion (Brian Duffy, ex-SAP, as new CRO).
	- **The market** the company is attacking: enterprise workflow management, ITSM, collaboration, enterprise AI search — collectively a \$50B+ TAM. Moving up-market from 85% Fortune 500 penetration (at only 10% of revenue) gives a long runway of ARPU expansion.

- ## Vibe Checks
	- **What do you like most?**
		- The Teamwork Graph is a genuine AI moat — it knows your org's context in ways a generic LLM cannot. Rovo agents built on this graph have a defensible position that Glean and others must fight uphill to replicate at scale. The 120%+ NRR and first \$1B Cloud quarter signal the enterprise up-market is working.
	- **What do you hate most?**
		- SBC is 23-26% of revenue — at 96% of FCF over the last 12 months, the free cash flow narrative is partially illusory. Jira's UX complexity is a real product liability: "fractal shit snowflakes" of configuration, Coinbase and Oscar Health partially migrating to Linear, Stack Overflow tracking decline from 55% → 47% usage in 2025. The \$1B+ Browser Company and DX acquisitions are head-scratchers.
	- **How popular is the product or service?**
		- Jira is #1 collaboration tool per Stack Overflow 2022-2024 surveys; 85% of Fortune 500 are customers. Hate is fashionable but rarely terminal — most users find it "fine" when properly configured. JSM wins competitive displacements from ServiceNow. But Rovo AI reviews are poor: "glorified search," virtual service agent "just kicks users to a form."

- ## Pre-Mortem — What Can Go Wrong?
	- 1. **Microsoft bundles "good enough" substitutes** into M365 at near-zero marginal cost, slowing Cloud seat expansion and compressing Marketplace take-rates. Azure DevOps + GitHub Issues + SharePoint + Copilot = the Jira + Confluence + JSM bundle, free.
	  2. **AI agent disruption of seat-based pricing** — if AI agents replace human seats, the pricing model breaks. Atlassian's defense is agent orchestration (assign tasks *to* agents in Jira), but this hasn't been monetized yet.
	  3. **Rovo doesn't close the gap** — current user reviews describe Rovo as "glorified search" that can't reliably answer questions from Confluence docs. If AI features don't mature quickly, the valuation premium erodes and Glean wins enterprise search.
	  4. **SBC dilution absorbs FCF** — SBC at 96% of FCF TTM means FCF is economically overstated. If share counts grow faster than revenue, intrinsic value per share compounds slowly.
	  5. **Data Center EOS execution risk** — DC is 25% of revenue and ends in 2029. If migrations stall or migrate to competitors (ServiceNow, Microsoft) rather than Atlassian Cloud, a cliff in revenue materializes.
	  6. **Integration risk from large M&A** — Browser Company (\$610M) and DX (\$1B) acquisitions are unproven; Loom was \$975M and is reportedly working, but two consecutive large bets increase execution risk.
	  7. **Commercial market deterioration** — SMB/mid-market sales data shows quota attainment 61%→52%, avg deal size \$120k→\$92k, PMF score declining. Enterprise improving, but commercial base must not collapse.

	- ### Expanded Anecdotes
		- The June 2026 Hacker News discussion about Jira being "Turing-complete" is a concentrated version of the Jira bear case. Commenters describe the product as slow, bloated, and over-customized, with one memorable framing that enterprise Jira instances become "fractal shit snowflakes" of configuration. The important investment read-through is not just that Jira has bad UX; it is that Jira's power comes from the same customizability that makes it painful. That creates a product paradox: the feature set is broad enough to satisfy management, process, compliance, and cross-functional reporting needs, but engineers often experience that breadth as bureaucracy.
		- The same HN thread also highlights the "target user" problem. Jira increasingly feels designed for managers, program managers, and process owners rather than engineers who want fast issue capture and lightweight workflows. This explains why Linear is repeatedly cited as the better developer tool: it is faster, cleaner, more keyboard-first, and easier for AI systems to parse because it has less redundant state and less schema complexity. The risk is that high-agency engineering teams peel off to Linear while Jira remains the official enterprise system of record.
		- The Jira automation anecdotes cut both ways. Several users describe scripting Jira, using Python CLIs, or using LLMs to paper over the product's defects. One user described maliciously using Jira as an exhaustive audit log to justify actions to management; others described shadow IT tools that bypassed the official UI and saved enough time to earn praise despite lacking formal sanction. These stories are bearish on UX but bullish on entrenchment: when users build tooling around a product they hate, it means the workflow is too embedded to abandon casually.
		- The worst first-hand HN feedback concerns Jira automation and Jira Align. One developer called Jira automation the worst programming experience they had ever had because of undocumented magic strings and API behavior that contradicted the web UI. Another described Jira Align as so painful they would rather do their own dental work without anesthetic. These anecdotes matter because Atlassian's up-market strategy depends on cross-functional portfolio, planning, and enterprise governance workflows; if the enterprise layers are uniquely painful, Atlassian may win the system-of-record seat while still inviting replacement at the team level.
		- The April 2026 Reddit JSM thread is more favorable on the core service-desk product. Users generally agree Jira Service Management is strong for ticketing, SLAs, queues, and IT service desk backbone use cases after years of production usage. The critique is focused on Atlassian's AI layer rather than JSM itself. The OP's Rovo trial felt like a search tool embedded in Atlassian, and the Virtual Service Agent mostly redirected users into forms that still created tickets for humans to process.
		- Rovo's early product feedback is materially weak. One user said Rovo could not reliably answer questions from their own Confluence docs half the time. Another JSM administrator managing 15-20 service desks tried to use Rovo to improve Slack-submitted ticket quality, but the workflow degraded into mandatory-field errors, permission errors, tickets created in the wrong service desks, OpenAI API errors, and cases where Rovo showed a user someone else's existing ticket instead of creating the intended one. The conclusion was "unusable." This is the cleanest product risk to monitor because the thesis relies on Rovo turning Teamwork Graph data into monetizable AI workflows, not just search.
		- Atlassian Team 2026 commentary reinforces that customers want Rovo to do real operational work, not simply answer questions. One attendee expected AI to be everywhere at the conference but still could not get Rovo to perform manual workflow-improvement tasks such as reviewing automations or suggesting workflow changes. This is the gap between AI positioning and user value: customers are asking for agentic administration of Jira/JSM complexity, but current feedback suggests Rovo is not yet reliable enough for that job.
		- The RepVue sales-org data suggests Atlassian is becoming two businesses at once. Commercial sellers are seeing quota attainment fall from 61% to 52%, average deal size fall from \$120K to \$92K, sales cycles lengthen from 116 to 141 days, and future outlook sentiment fall from 3.82 to 2.77. Enterprise sellers show the opposite pattern: quota attainment rose from 49% to 63%, average deal size rose from \$305K to \$413K, sales cycles compressed from 227 to 195 days, and future outlook improved from 2.75 to 3.14. This supports management's decision to self-fund more AI and enterprise sales investment, but it also raises a question: is the PLG/commercial engine weakening while enterprise temporarily masks it?
		- Stack Overflow survey data is an important contrary signal for developer mindshare. Jira was the #1 collaboration tool from 2022-2024 with roughly 52-55% usage, but fell to 46.8% in 2025. Confluence declined from 42.2% to 33%, and Trello declined from 35.3% in 2022 to 13.8% in 2025. Retention for all three products reportedly fell sharply in 2025 by roughly 10-13 percentage points. The caveat is that survey samples can shift year to year, but the direction is consistent with developer frustration and the rise of cleaner alternatives.
		- The "Jira hate is overblown" counterpoint remains important. A recurring expert view is that Jira is highly dependent on configuration quality: simple, lightweight setup can make it effective, while complex workflows make it unbearable. One expert take was blunt: Jira is great, and people should get over themselves; usefulness is a function of setup quality and team buy-in. This is bullish for retention because many complaints are implementation failures rather than product failures, but bearish for customer experience because Atlassian does not fully control how customers configure the product.
		- Linear migration anecdotes show the credible bear case. Oscar Health moved 600 people in a regulated healthcare environment from Jira to Linear in about one month after reportedly hitting Jira's custom-field ceiling; leadership visibility improved and the VP of Engineering said everyone seemed happier with Linear. The caveat from Reddit commenters is that Oscar reportedly still had more than 3,000 people on Jira, so this was not a full-enterprise replacement. The more precise read is that Linear can win high-value engineering teams even inside regulated companies, while Jira may remain the broader compliance and enterprise workflow layer.
		- Coinbase is a similar partial-displacement signal. Linear became the default planning tool for PED teams after a pilot where 90% of users preferred it, but regulated workflows such as security bugs, incidents, TRRs, and other control-heavy work stayed in Jira. A Coinbase engineer's read was that Jira was designed for older software-development workflows with more friction around points and sprints, while modern teams need project bases, routine updates, OKR tracking, impact metrics, and AI-friendly ticket generation. The investment read-through is nuanced: Jira is not losing all workflow, but the highest-velocity developer planning workflows may be shifting to Linear.
		- The macro backdrop matters for interpreting Atlassian's multiple compression. Software multiples compressed from 2022-2025 because interest rates rose, COVID-era seat growth was overbought, and SBC consumed too much of the revenue-growth story in parts of SaaS. That means Atlassian's stock decline is not solely a product-quality verdict or AI disruption signal. The product anecdotes matter, but they should be weighed alongside broad SaaS de-rating and investor fatigue with high-SBC software names.
