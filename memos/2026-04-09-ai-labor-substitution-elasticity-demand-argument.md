- ## AI Labor Displacement — Why Aggregate Demand Doesn't Automatically Collapse
	- **Source**: Analyst/research argument, summarized April 2026
	- **Thesis**: Even in a high-AI-substitution scenario, aggregate demand collapse is not the automatic outcome — multiple structural buffers constrain the worst case

- ## Core Argument

	- **The key variable is substitution elasticity** — how easily and cheaply firms can replace human labor with AI capital
		- High elasticity → labor's income share collapses, capital share surges → inequality widens sharply
		- But high capital income ≠ demand destruction: profits get reinvested, distributed as dividends, taxed, or spent — spending velocity is lower than wages but not zero
	- **Demand collapse requires two simultaneous failures**: redistribution mechanisms breaking down *and* investment opportunities drying up at the same time — an extreme and unlikely combination
	- **Democratic policy response acts as a natural brake**: governments facing mass labor displacement would be expected to respond with regulatory limits on substitution and fiscal transfers — this endogenously caps how far substitution elasticity can run in practice
	- **Empirical reality check**: as of today, there is no visible AI disruption in labor market data — forward-looking labor market indicators have actually *improved*, and AI data center construction is driving a measurable pickup in construction hiring

- ## Investment Implications
	- The "AI kills demand → recession" bear case requires a chain of failures that markets tend to price as tail risk, not base case
	- AI capex buildout is currently *adding* jobs (construction, infrastructure) — the near-term labor market effect is expansionary, not contractionary
	- Capital income recycling (investment, dividends, taxes) sustains consumption even in a high-substitution scenario — the transition shock may be distributional rather than aggregate
	- Policy risk is real but symmetric: regulation that limits AI substitution also limits AI productivity gains, creating a policy trade-off that democratic governments will manage gradually rather than ignore
	- Near-term macro conclusion: AI displacement is not yet a demand headwind; it is a supply-side productivity story with demand-side upside from capex spending
