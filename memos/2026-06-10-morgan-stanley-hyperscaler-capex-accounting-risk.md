- **Source**: User-provided text and screenshots from a Morgan Stanley report on hyperscaler AI capex accounting and obligations.
- **Theme**: The unsettling part of the AI infrastructure boom is not only the size of hyperscaler capex, but the way future cash obligations are being engineered through purchase commitments, leases not yet commenced, RPO, supplier financing, construction-in-progress accounting, and subjective lease/service classifications.

- ## Bottom Line
	- The report argues that hyperscaler AI capex is creating a large shadow liability stack that is not visible in simple debt or reported earnings metrics.
	- The key accounting issue is timing:
		- cash commitments are made today
		- assets sit in construction-in-progress before depreciation fully hits earnings
		- future leases and purchase obligations sit outside headline debt
		- suppliers finance part of the buildout through stretched payables
	- The main investment implication is that reported profitability may overstate steady-state economics during the build phase, while future depreciation and cash outflows are already being locked in.

- ## Accounting Mechanisms
	- **Construction in progress delays the earnings hit**
		- During the buildout, large spending can sit in construction in progress before the asset is placed into service.
		- That means current earnings can look better than the eventual run-rate economics because depreciation has not fully started.
		- The report's warning is that the capex boom creates a delayed depreciation wave rather than an immediate income-statement hit.
	- **Leases not yet commenced hide future balance-sheet expansion**
		- Lease commitments may be contracted before the lease begins.
		- Until commencement, the obligation can sit in footnotes rather than appearing as a fully recognized lease liability.
		- This makes leverage look lower during the commitment phase.
	- **Purchase commitments are not debt, but still constrain future cash**
		- Purchase commitments are legally or commercially meaningful future outflows even if they do not show up as debt.
		- The issue is not technical insolvency; it is that future optionality is reduced when nearly `$1T` of purchases is already committed.
	- **Supplier financing masks cash pressure**
		- Rising unpaid capex and stretched DPO mean suppliers are absorbing part of the working-capital burden.
		- This can improve buyer cash flow temporarily but pushes stress into the supply chain.
	- **Lease-versus-service classification is a gray zone**
		- GPU and infrastructure contracts can be structured as leases or services depending on control, substitution rights, asset specificity, and contract economics.
		- That classification can shift billions of obligations on or off the balance sheet.
		- The accounting flexibility matters most when investors compare cloud companies using debt, depreciation, EBITDA, and free-cash-flow metrics.

- ## Oracle: The Aggressive Case
	- Oracle is the most aggressive case in the report's framing.
	- The concern is not only that Oracle is growing AI infrastructure exposure quickly, but that the growth appears to rely on the most financially stretched version of the model:
		- very large lease commitments
		- RPO growth above `300%`
		- capex-to-sales reaching `189%`
		- depreciation ratio rising from `7%` to `28%`
		- DPO expanding from `35` to `170` days
	- **Read-through**: Oracle may be running the highest financial leverage in the AI infrastructure ecosystem, even if not all of that leverage is visible as conventional debt.
	- **Bull case**: if contracted AI demand turns into durable utilization and high-margin cloud revenue, Oracle's aggressive posture can create a major scale catch-up.
	- **Bear case**: if demand, pricing, utilization, or customer credit quality disappoints, Oracle faces the worst combination of future depreciation, lease commitments, supplier financing reversal, and reduced balance-sheet flexibility.

- ## Investment Read-Through
	- **For hyperscalers**
		- The AI capex cycle is partly being financed through contractual obligations rather than only current-period cash capex and debt issuance.
		- Investors should track purchase commitments, leases not yet commenced, RPO, DPO, CIP, and depreciation schedules alongside headline capex.
		- The key risk is not a sudden accounting surprise, but a multi-year margin and free-cash-flow grind as assets enter service.
	- **For Nvidia and semiconductor suppliers**
		- Purchase commitments and unpaid capex show that customers are locking in demand, which is bullish for near-term backlog and pricing power.
		- The risk is that the supply chain becomes exposed to buyer financing stress if cloud customers stretch payables or renegotiate obligations.
	- **For Oracle**
		- Oracle is the highest-beta expression of the AI capex accounting trade.
		- Reported cloud growth and RPO strength must be weighed against lease commitments, future depreciation, supplier financing, and capex intensity.
	- **For memory / AI infrastructure**
		- The report supports the idea that AI infrastructure demand is real and contracted, but also raises the hurdle for returns.
		- If accounting timing has flattered current earnings, hardware suppliers may still win while infrastructure buyers absorb lower long-term returns.

- ## What To Track Next
	| Metric | Why it matters |
	|---|---|
	| Leases not yet commenced | Shows future balance-sheet obligations before lease start |
	| Purchase commitments | Captures locked-in supplier spend that is not debt |
	| RPO growth versus cash conversion | Separates contracted demand from realized economics |
	| Construction in progress | Indicates how much capex has not yet begun depreciating |
	| Depreciation as a share of cloud revenue / operating income | Measures the delayed earnings burden |
	| DPO and unpaid capex | Shows whether suppliers are financing the buildout |
	| Lease-versus-service disclosures | Reveals whether GPU/cloud obligations are recognized on balance sheet |
	| Capex-to-sales | Identifies companies whose infrastructure buildout is running ahead of current revenue scale |

- ## Caveats
	- The screenshot data is visually extracted from report exhibits and should be treated as directional where labels are not explicitly shown.
	- Purchase commitments, leases, and RPO are not all equivalent to debt. They differ in enforceability, duration, cancellation terms, customer pass-through, and revenue backing.
	- Off-balance-sheet does not mean hidden from filings; the point is that common screening metrics can miss the scale and timing of the obligations.
	- Aggressive financial engineering can be rational if AI demand is durable and utilization is high. The risk is asymmetry: the obligations are fixed earlier than the returns are proven.
