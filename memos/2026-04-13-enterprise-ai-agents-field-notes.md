- ## Enterprise AI Agents — Field Notes from IT/AI Leader Meetings
	- **Source**: Industry field notes, investor/consultant road trip meeting with dozens of IT and AI leaders across banking, media, retail, healthcare, consulting, tech, and sports (2026-04-13)
	- **Headline**: Enterprises are transitioning from chat-era AI to agentic AI, but change management, legacy systems, and compute budgeting are the dominant friction points

- ## Key Takeaways

	- **Chat → Agents**: Clear directional shift from chat-era AI to agents that use tools, process data, and execute real work. Enterprises simultaneously evolving from "let a thousand flowers bloom" adoption to targeted automation of specific workflows
	- **Change management is the bottleneck**: Most workflows aren't set up to drop agents directly in. Enterprises need extensive internal and partner support. One company has a head of AI in every business unit reporting to a central team just to keep functions coordinated
	- **"Tokenmaxxing"**: Most enterprises operate on locked annual OpEx budgets — they are having real trade-off discussions on how to budget for tokens. One company pitched a "shark tank" model for compute budget allocation; others are building a hierarchy of needs to ration compute to the best internal use-cases
	- **Legacy systems are a critical blocker**: Decades of on-prem systems and cloud migrations without true modernization mean agents cannot easily tap into data sources in a unified way. Modernization is now a top priority specifically to unblock agentic use-cases
	- **Not about replacing jobs**: Major use-cases are things companies couldn't do before or couldn't prioritize — software upgrades, back-office automation, large-scale document processing for business insights. Emphasis is on revenue-generating use-cases over cost-cutting
	- **Headless software is the new enterprise requirement**: Enterprises demand all software works across any set of agents they choose. Vendors that don't make this technically or economically easy will get dropped
	- **Standardization is paralyzed by speed of change**: No one wants to lock into the wrong architecture. Multi-agent world = interoperability is paramount. Companies accept they're operating across multiple competing agent systems simultaneously
	- **Everyone is working more, not less**: AI is not reducing workload right now — teams report being busier than ever, mirroring Silicon Valley's experience

- ## Meta-Observation: Engineers Remain Essential
	- Despite Silicon Valley's narrative that AI makes hard things easy, the most powerful agentic use-cases are *more* technical than prior software eras — Skills, MCP, CLIs, etc. are simple concepts for tech but esoteric in the real world
	- Diffusion will take real time and work
	- Engineering headcount estimates are wrong in both directions: engineers may not *write* software as much, but they will set up and operate the agentic systems that automate enterprise work — demand for technical operators goes up, not down

- ## Investment Implications
	- **Positive for SaaS platforms with strong APIs and agent compatibility** (ServiceNow, Atlassian, Salesforce): "headless software" requirement rewards incumbents who open up their systems to agents; vendors who resist will churn
	- **Positive for systems integrators and implementation partners**: Change management and legacy modernization are massive unmet needs; enterprises need external help at scale
	- **Positive for compute/inference infrastructure**: Token budgeting pressure is a demand signal, not a demand cap — enterprises are rationing because they want more, not because they don't see value
	- **Skeptical of "AI replaces engineers" narrative**: Field evidence shows engineers are needed more, not less, to operate agentic infrastructure in enterprises
	- **Risk to low-end / non-interoperable SaaS vendors**: Enterprises will kick out vendors that don't support multi-agent interoperability — pressure on fragmented, closed tooling
