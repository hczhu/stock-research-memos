- ## Korea–China HBM Gap Narrows to ~Three Years (CXMT's All-Out Push)
	- **Source**: Korean semiconductor-industry press, reported on the 1st (June 2026)
	- **Thesis**: China's government has issued an "all-out HBM mobilization order," and CXMT has chased Korean HBM3 "right up to the chin" — narrowing the Korea–China HBM tech gap to roughly three years. Yield, not core technology, is now CXMT's binding constraint. The volume offensive matters even if the tech lags.

- ## Key Data Points

	| Metric | Value |
	|---|---|
	| Korea–China HBM tech gap | ~3 years (assessed "more than 3 years" given Korea is already on HBM4) |
	| CXMT HBM level vs. Korea | Lags Samsung by 2–3 generations; has reached Samsung/SK Hynix **HBM3** level |
	| CXMT constraint | **Yield (good-product share) is low** — but the technology itself has caught up to Korean HBM3 |
	| Korea's current node | Already in mass production of **6th-gen HBM4** |
	| CXMT 12-inch wafer capacity (YE 2026E) | **~300,000 wafers/month** |
	| CXMT share of global supply | **~14%** — "shoulder to shoulder" with Micron |

	- **Generation framing**: HBM3 is referred to as the 4th-generation HBM; HBM4 as the 6th generation. Korea mass-producing HBM4 while CXMT reaches HBM3 = the ~3-year (or more) gap

- ## What Changed vs. Prior View
	- Prior industry view: CXMT had effectively **abandoned HBM3** development after hitting a technical wall
	- Revised view: CXMT did **not** give up — it pushed aggressively and rose to Samsung/SK Hynix HBM3 level; only **yield is low**, the underlying technology has caught up
	- Chinese government is **pressing CXMT to sharply expand HBM mass-production capacity**; CXMT building wafer capacity aggressively in response

- ## NVIDIA N1X / RTX Spark — Korean Memory Demand Tailwind
	- At **GTC Taipei** (Taipei Music Center), Jensen Huang unveiled NVIDIA's first PC AI chip, the **N1X**, alongside the **NVIDIA RTX Spark** notebook lineup (with Microsoft)
	- The N1X carries Samsung's and SK Hynix's high-performance low-power memory, **LPDDR5X** → fresh surge in Korean memory-chip demand
	- (Cross-reference: SoCAMM2 / LPDDR5X demand dynamics in [[2026-06-04-nvidia-socamm2-module-capacity-adjustment-meritz]]; AI-PC product critique in [[2026-06-04-stratechery-nvidia-ai-pc-microsoft-solara-mai]])

- ## Investment Implications
	- **Supply-side risk is real but bounded near-term**: CXMT reaching ~14% of global 12-inch wafer supply (≈Micron-scale volume) is a meaningful commodity-DRAM/low-end-HBM threat, but the **3-generation HBM gap + low yield** means it is not yet qualified competition for hyperscale HBM4 where Samsung/SK Hynix earn the premium
	- **Yield is the swing variable**: The bear case for the memory super-cycle hinges partly on CXMT's state-subsidized, price-insensitive supply (see [[2026-06-02-memory-cycle-bear-case-arguments]] and [[2026-06-03-china-memory-cxmt-ymtc-geopolitics]]). This report says the tech has caught up to HBM3 — so the question shifts to how fast CXMT closes the yield gap and whether it disrupts commodity DRAM / low-end HBM pricing
	- **Korea's HBM4 lead preserves the high-margin tier**: As long as Samsung/SK Hynix stay 2–3 generations ahead on the products hyperscalers actually buy, the two-tier market (premium AI HBM vs. commodity) holds — CXMT pressures the bottom, not the top, of the stack
	- **Geopolitical accelerant**: A state "mobilization order" decouples CXMT capex from commercial ROI — the classic concern that subsidized Chinese supply lands at scale just as the cycle matures (2027–2028)
	- **Korean dual read**: Same Korean makers face the CXMT threat at the low end *and* a fresh LPDDR5X demand pull from NVIDIA's N1X at the high end — net structurally favorable while the HBM4 lead holds
