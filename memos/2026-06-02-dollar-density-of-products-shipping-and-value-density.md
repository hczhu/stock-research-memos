	- **What the chart is showing**:
		- x-axis is `dollars per cubic meter`
		- y-axis is `dollars per kilogram`

	- **Why it matters**:
		- high dollar-density products can absorb shipping, warehousing, insurance, and tariff costs more easily
		- low dollar-density products are more exposed to freight inflation, storage costs, and geographic manufacturing disadvantages
		- this matters for stock work in categories like consumer electronics, hard goods retail, appliances, autos, and commodity-like household products

- ## Read-Through
	- The chart is a useful reminder that not all physical products are equally constrained by the real-world cost of moving atoms.
	- Low-density categories like furniture, paper goods, litter, and large appliances tend to have:
		- lower gross-margin resilience against freight spikes
		- more incentive for local warehousing or regionalized supply chains
		- less room to use air freight except in special cases
	- High-density categories like premium electronics and precious metals tend to have:
		- more flexibility in manufacturing location
		- more tolerance for tariffs and expedited shipping
		- lower logistics cost as a percentage of selling price

- ## Stock Implications
	- For `consumer electronics`, high value density is one reason global supply chains can remain viable even when transport costs rise.
	- For `furniture`, `appliances`, and other bulky hard goods, freight and storage are not side issues; they are part of the core unit-economics equation.
	- For `autos`, the chart suggests an intermediate position: vehicles are far more valuable than staples, but still much less dense than phones, chips, jewelry, or software-adjacent hardware.
	- As a framework, dollar density helps explain which industries are naturally more global and which ones are structurally more regional.
