- **Source**: Morgan Stanley Global Insight — "Chipflation: Navigating A Memory Crisis", June 2, 2026
- **Thesis**: Surging memory prices and supply scarcity are a cross-sector risk as AI reprices a critical input across the digital economy. What began as an AI infrastructure bottleneck is now spreading into hardware margins, device affordability, cloud costs, inflation, and policy.
- **Key claims**
	- DRAM prices have risen more than **6x** over the last year — a sharp discontinuity from decades of deflationary pricing (price per Gb fell ~10x every 5 years from 1957–2020)
	- Memory TAM surging from **$220B in 2025 → $890B in 2026E** (~4x); in 3 months alone expectations for 2026 memory market revenue increased 71%, from $520B to $890B
	- Incremental memory revenue in 2026 (~$600B) is larger than the entire smartphone TAM ($600B), server TAM ($424B), PC TAM ($256B) individually
	- Electronics components PPI: **+30% Y/Y** as of April 2026; headline CPI impact <1% (small basket weight)
	- Memory accounts for **5–70% of hardware product BOM**: AFA Storage 73%, High-End Server 53%, AI Server 25%, General Purpose Server 40%, PC 25%, Smartphone 18%, Printers 5%

	- The speed of revision (+71% in 3 months) reflects how rapidly the AI demand signal is repricing consensus — not a gradual upgrade cycle but a step-function repricing event

- **Why this cycle is structural, not cyclical**
	- Supply takes years to build, qualify, and ramp — tool capacity to qualified output is a **1–2 year process** (AI demand inflects Q0 → HBM/DRAM available Q7/8)
	- AI demand is scaling across three layers at once: more memory per chip, more chips per system, more systems per cluster (7x chip, 65x system, 1,800x cluster in HBM since A100)
	- Mobile AI agents and physical AI agents represent another large demand wave not yet in consensus models
	- LTAs (Long-Term Agreements) are replacing spot pricing; hyperscalers lock in supply first; residual pool shrinks for everyone else
	- Post-COVID lesson: Intel and Samsung deferred EUV orders after demand weakened, creating order volatility — this cycle memory makers need LTAs/prepayments before building capacity
- **What could challenge the thesis**
	- AI capex slows down (reduced LLM competition, cost of funding infrastructure)
	- Dramatic improvements in model memory efficiency (KV cache, tokenization)
	- Power/electricity availability constraints slow AI data center buildouts
	- Demand destruction in consumer/industrial hardware eases supply tightness
	- Geopolitics and trade policy disruptions to global supply chain
