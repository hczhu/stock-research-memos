- **Source**: Morgan Stanley "Chipflation" report, June 2, 2026 — Section: Chips, Growth and Inflation – A Macro Perspective
- **Core argument**: Chipflation is clearly visible in PPI but has a muted effect on headline CPI due to small basket weights; the bigger macroeconomic risk is the indirect transmission through corporate margins, cloud bills, capex budgets, and delayed technology deployment
- **PPI impact — where chipflation is most visible**
	- US PPI: Electronic Components and Accessories (Y/Y): **+27.6%** as of April 2026 — near all-time high, no clear top yet
	- Rise spreading across supply chain: Stage 2 intermediate demand goods inputs (+7.1% Y/Y) and Stage 3 intermediate demand goods inputs (+24.4% Y/Y)
	- Electronics/components contribute less than 1% to headline PPI, so inflationary push reduces at headline level — but the effect is clear in subsectors
	- PPI vs CPI divergence: CPI measures computers ~5% Y/Y (quality-adjusted) vs PPI components ~30% → gap implies producers are absorbing in margins rather than passing through to consumers (for now)
- **CPI impact — modest but measurable**
	- Total estimated impact on headline CPI: **~0.10pp** in 2026 (from memory costs alone)
	- PC and smartphone combined: **0.08pp** on headline CPI
	- Breakdown by component (memory cost effect on CPI in 2026):

		| Component | Memory Cost Effect | CPI Weight | CPI Contribution (pp) |
		|---|---:|---:|---:|
		| PCs | 15pp | 0.30% | 0.045 |
		| Smartphones | 15pp | 0.20% | 0.030 |
		| TVs | 10pp | 0.10% | 0.010 |
		| Cars | 0pp (absorbed in margins) | 3.80% | 0 |
		| Major household appliances | 5pp | 0.06% | ~0.003 |
		| Game consoles | 125pp | 0.01% | negligible |
	- ASP-to-CPI relationship is not 1-to-1: quality adjustments make CPI series smoother; historically a 30% Y/Y increase in ASPs broadly consistent with ~10% Y/Y increase in CPI prices for computers
	- MS forecasts CPI computers at **10% Y/Y in 2026** (consistent with 30% Y/Y ASP) — an acceleration of 15pp vs 2025
- **Why PPI-to-CPI divergence matters for macro**
	- Gap between PPI (+30%) and CPI (~5–10%) for electronics implies difficult margin decisions being made by consumer-facing producers
	- The longer chip prices stay elevated, the more macro consequences (more margin pressure flows into corporate profits, restricts investment, compresses productivity)
	- AI capex has been orthogonal to macroeconomic conditions, but if it slows because chip costs become too high, it has the double impact of slowing AI investment AND delaying translation to AI productivity
- **Macro channels beyond CPI (the bigger story)**
	- **Corporate margins**: memory inflation flowing directly through COGS for non-hyperscaler hardware companies
	- **Cloud bills**: enterprises paying chipflation indirectly via AWS/Azure/GCP pricing — every workload running on cloud infrastructure is exposed without knowing it
	- **Capex budgets**: hardware budgets being revised upward (10% of CIOs buying mission-critical only and deferring non-critical upgrades); constraining IT investment velocity
	- **Delayed technology deployment**: higher memory costs pushing back ADAS rollouts in autos, medical device upgrades, industrial automation
	- **Productivity effects**: insufficient chip supply can delay data center projects, slow cloud development, increase enterprise costs — ultimately affecting productivity growth
- **Regional macro effects**
	- Memory concentration strengthens position of memory-producing economies: Samsung, SK hynix, Micron together control ~90% of DRAM market and 100% of HBM market
	- South Korea produces nearly 75% of global DRAM chips → surge in memory revenues substantially bolsters national GDP and local stock indices
	- Global consumer electronics stocks: fallen 1% on average YTD; global memory makers: risen ~300% YTD, alongside unprecedented EPS revisions of 333% YTD
- **MS inflation forecasts**
	- Expect further upside pressure in consumer indices as higher-cost memory flows through inventory and COGS pipelines
	- Effect structurally lower than what is seen in PPI because of relatively low chips share in overall cost structure and consumer absorption capacity
	- Key risk: if AI capex slows due to chip cost concerns, it could slow the AI-investment narrative that has been a central pillar of the US bull market
