- ## Main Argument
	- The chart frames the `AI shock` as structurally similar to the `China shock`.
	- In both cases, the first-order effect is obvious displacement in the directly exposed segment of the labor market:
		- manufacturing workers during the China shock
		- knowledge workers and middle-skill white-collar workers during the AI shock
	- The core bullish macro argument is that national unemployment can stay low even while painful displacement happens inside specific sectors or communities, because offsetting forces elsewhere in the economy absorb a large share of the blow.

- ## Stock Memo Context
	- This framework is relevant for investing because it separates `headline unemployment risk` from `sector-level disruption risk`.
	- If the analogy holds, the labor market may look healthier at the aggregate level than bottom-up anecdotes suggest, which matters for:
		- software and AI beneficiaries tied to productivity and business formation
		- cyclical and consumer names whose demand depends more on macro employment than on job composition
		- firms selling tools that let incumbents absorb labor displacement through internal reorganization rather than pure layoffs
	- The memo's implicit investment conclusion is pro-growth:
		- AI may destroy jobs in exposed categories
		- but lower cognitive labor costs, higher productivity, and new firm formation can expand total output enough to offset much of the damage in national statistics

- ## Caveats
	- The chart itself presents an analogy, not a proof.
	- The `hidden unemployment` box on the China side is important because it argues that low headline unemployment can still mask deeper labor market pain.
	- The `winners and losers` box on the AI side matters for stock picking:
		- AI may be positive for aggregate growth
		- but the gains will not be evenly distributed across workers, industries, or business models
	- The screenshot cites a wide mix of academic and institutional sources, but this memo only extracts the visible claims in the chart and the user-provided context rather than validating each source independently.

- ## Memo Takeaway
	- The chart's main message is not that AI will be painless.
	- It is that the economy can experience sharp displacement in specific white-collar roles while still showing resilient national unemployment, because productivity, business formation, and gradual adoption create offsetting demand elsewhere.
	- For investing, that argues for distinguishing between:
		- `macro labor collapse`, which the chart argues is unlikely
		- and `micro labor disruption`, which is likely already underway and can still create large relative winners and losers.
