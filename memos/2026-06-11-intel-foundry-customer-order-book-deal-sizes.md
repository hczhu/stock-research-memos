- ## Intel Foundry — Customer Order Book & Deal Sizes
	- **Source**: user-provided commentary/note on Intel Foundry's order book and financing (editorial; opinion portions flagged below).
	- **Setup**: A large anchor customer commit (Terafab partners — **SpaceX & Tesla**) **solves Intel's 14A capacity issue** and triggers the build. CEO **Lip-Bu Tan** had publicly told the market he would **shut Foundry down if no customers showed up** — now one has, so "it's time to build." A visible, signed demand book is far cheaper for capital markets to fund than a turnaround promise — "an equity raise sold against signed demand prices very differently from one sold against a promise."

- ## Author's Take (opinion — flagged)
	- The thesis: **issue equity now** to fund the ramp — "undo a decade of financial engineering." The argument is that the **equity-issuance window is the widest it's been in a while**, signed demand de-risks the raise, and at a ~$498B cap a $25B follow-on is easily absorbed (if Cerebras can raise $5.55B, "Intel can raise 25"). Framed as **the critical moment for silicon sovereignty** — "time to press."

- ## Investment Implications
	- **The anchor de-risks the capex story but adds it back to the bill**: tens of $B of incremental Intel matching capital re-enters the model — watch for the **equity raise** as the funding mechanism (dilution vs. a de-risked, demand-backed ramp).
	- **Order-book quality > promise**: Nvidia (Xeon 6 host), Google (Xeon + IPUs), SambaNova (inference) plus the Terafab/SpaceX/Tesla 14A anchor give Intel a **signed book to point at** — the key variable capital markets price.
	- **14A is the gating node**: the SpaceX/Tesla commit specifically **solves 14A capacity**; the 100K→1M WSPM ramp is the execution (and capital) risk.
