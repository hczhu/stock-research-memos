- ## Meta's Tent Data Centers and the Rise of Behind-the-Meter Compute
	- **Source**: Cleanview report on behind-the-meter data centers; author reviewed hundreds of documents and satellite images, June 2026
	- **Thesis**: Meta has abandoned conventional data center construction in favor of rapid-deployment tent structures powered by off-grid turbines — cutting build time in half. Behind-the-meter data center capacity is growing from 2 GW today to potentially 13 GW by end of 2027, equivalent to NYC's entire power demand

- ## Behind-the-Meter (BTM) Data Center Capacity Forecast

	| Timeframe | BTM Capacity | Equivalent |
	|---|---:|---|
	| Today (mid-2026) | ~2 GW | — |
	| End of 2026 | ~3 GW | ~3 nuclear power plants |
	| End of 2027 | Up to 13 GW | > NYC total power demand |

	- BTM capacity is data center power sourced directly from on-site generation (turbines, generators, jet engines) rather than the utility grid
	- The 2→13 GW trajectory over 18 months represents a ~6.5× increase — faster than any grid-connected capacity expansion could achieve given interconnection timelines
	- Meta's tent strategy is explicitly cited as one of the primary drivers of BTM growth speed

- ## Why Tents + Off-Grid Power

	- **Interconnection bottleneck bypassed**: Grid-connected data centers face 3–7 year interconnection queues in most US markets; BTM gas turbines can be permitted and built in 12–18 months
	- **Construction speed**: Tent structures with prefabricated modular components take months vs. 2–3 years for permanent buildings — Meta's New Albany campus demonstrates the delta
	- **Zuckerberg's timeline pressure**: With NVIDIA GPU allocations at stake and competitors building at speed, compute-online velocity is more valuable than architectural elegance
	- **Cost trade-off accepted**: Off-grid gas turbines have higher $/MWh than grid power but eliminate the largest bottleneck (interconnection) entirely

- ## Investment Implications
	- **Williams Companies**: A 10-year, 400 MW power deal with Meta is a material contract — Williams is typically a midstream gas infrastructure company; this signals hyperscalers are pulling gas infrastructure players directly into the AI compute supply chain
	- **Gas turbine manufacturers** (GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Baker Hughes): BTM data centers run on gas turbines; if 13 GW of BTM capacity is online by 2027, that implies significant incremental turbine orders beyond what utility customers are placing
	- **Conventional data center construction timelines are no longer the binding constraint**: The 2–3 year build cycle that was considered irreducible has been halved — this accelerates the cadence at which AI compute can be deployed and compresses the visibility window for infrastructure investors
	- **Grid operators and utilities face a structural bypass**: BTM growth at this rate means hyperscaler load — the largest single source of new electricity demand — is increasingly not showing up on utility demand forecasts or grid interconnection queues
	- **Regulatory risk**: Tent structures and off-grid turbines likely face evolving zoning, emissions, and permitting scrutiny — city permits were required for the New Albany tents, creating a new regulatory surface area
	- **Meta capex efficiency signal**: If tents + turbines are becoming the standard Meta playbook, Meta's $/GPU-deployed ratio is improving materially vs. peers still building conventional facilities — this could compress Meta's AI infrastructure cost per token relative to slower-building competitors
