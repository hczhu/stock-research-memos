- ## Michael Burry's Bear Case Against Memory Companies
	- **Source**:
		- user-provided writeup titled `Misremembering Memory Chips`
		- the writeup discloses a large `SOXX` put position with strikes in the low-to-mid `$300s` and expiries in early `2027`
	- **Thesis**:
		- Burry argues the current memory boom is a classic cyclical top disguised as a structural AI shortage.
		- His core view is that HBM and AI demand do not repeal the memory industry's commodity economics: low-cost production wins, capacity additions overshoot, margins crash, and apparently cheap cyclicals re-rate lower when peak earnings roll over.
	- **Context**:
		- the memory bull case says HBM, AI inference, long-term agreements, and tight supply have structurally changed the cycle
		- Burry's rebuttal is that those same features are exactly how bullwhip cycles begin: customer fear creates inflated order books, supplier certainty drives factory buildouts, and eventual demand normalization leaves excess capacity

- ## Burry's Bullwhip Logic
	| Step | Description | Bear interpretation |
	| --- | --- | --- |
	| `1. AI demand shock` | HBM demand rises sharply from AI training and inference. | Real demand starts the shortage. |
	| `2. HBM reallocates DRAM capacity` | Producers shift commodity DRAM front-end die and cleanroom capacity toward HBM. | Conventional DDR5 supply tightens, lifting margins across the memory stack. |
	| `3. Customers panic-buy future supply` | Customers bring forward 2027 demand because they fear shortages. | Order books become inflated by hoarding, not only end demand. |
	| `4. Suppliers build greenfield capacity` | High conviction plus LTAs justify new lines and cleanroom space. | The LTA / prebooking structure becomes the mechanism of oversupply. |
	| `5. HBM pricing catches up or demand steadies` | HBM margins normalize versus conventional DRAM, while efficiency gains may reduce future memory intensity. | Order urgency fades and excess capacity appears. |
	| `6. Commodity pricing returns` | Spare HBM capacity and DRAM capacity pressure prices. | Margins crash and memory stocks revert to cyclical multiples. |

- ## Why Burry Thinks Current P/Es Are Misleading
	- His view is that memory companies are being valued on peak earnings.
	- In cyclical industries, low P/E ratios at peak margins can be value traps because the `E` collapses first.
	- He argues that when memory investors rediscover cyclicality, stocks will re-rate to cyclical multiples on normalized or trough earnings rather than current run-rate earnings.
	- The key call is not just lower margins; it is that the market will stop treating current earnings as durable.

- ## Investment Implications
	- The memo is a direct challenge to the memory super-cycle thesis.
	- Burry is not saying AI demand is fake; he is saying the memory market will overbuild against a real demand signal.
	- The most important watch point is whether 2027 bookings represent genuine end demand or fear-driven allocation hoarding.
	- His thesis is most bearish for `Micron`, `Samsung`, `SK Hynix`, and memory-heavy semiconductor ETFs, but the expressed trade is through `SOXX` puts.
	- The debate should focus less on whether HBM is important and more on whether current margins and order books are durable.

- ## Memo Takeaway
	- Burry's argument is that HBM is not a new permanent pricing regime; it is the latest demand shock in the most cyclical part of technology.
	- The core bear case is: AI creates shortage, shortage creates hoarding, hoarding creates factory commitments, factory commitments create oversupply, and oversupply crashes margins.
