- ## Summary
	- DRAM prices are surging driven by AI infrastructure demand, while mobile handset demand is collapsing — creating a bifurcated outcome where memory makers win and mobile chip vendors/device OEMs lose hard
	- The two dynamics are linked: HBM/server DRAM capacity concentration is crowding out conventional DRAM supply, which raises costs across mobile and PC, further compressing already-thin margins in consumer devices

- ## DRAM Pricing: Sustained Upward Momentum
	- Samsung raised Q2 DRAM average contract prices ~**30% QoQ**, following a ~**100% QoQ increase in Q1**

		| Period | Contract Price (KRW) | QoQ Change |
		|---|---:|---:|
		| Start of 2025 | ~10,000 | Baseline |
		| Q1 2026 | ~20,000 | +~100% |
		| Q2 2026 | ~26,000 | +~30% |
	- Samsung completed Q2 supply negotiations with major customers in late March; contracts are finalized
	- **HBM and server DRAM**: demand firm, no signs of price stabilization or decline
	- **Legacy/PC DRAM (DDR4 8Gb)**: flat MoM as of late March per DRAMeXchange — rate of increase decelerating for legacy nodes
	- **DDR5 and next-gen DRAM**: prices continue climbing steadily per industry consensus
	- SK Hynix and Micron expected to follow Samsung with similar ~30% Q2 price increases
	- **Key inflection = Q3 2026**: production capacity at the Big 3 has not expanded dramatically; AI-driven demand remains the governing factor for price trajectory
	- Root cause: Big 3 memory makers concentrated capacity on HBM → conventional DRAM supply tightened → broad price spike across server, PC, and mobile categories
	- "Today's memory price is the cheapest it will ever be" — industry adage still holds for now

- ## Mobile Handset Market: Structural Demand Collapse
	- Channel checks (Qualcomm insider): mid-range Snapdragon production being cut hard — **some SKU-level yearly projections down ~30%**
	- Memory and flash prices are the primary demand destructor in the mid-tier phone segment

	| Year | Estimated Shipments | YoY Change |
	|---|---:|---|
	| 2025 (base) | ~1.2B units | — |
	| 2026E | ~0.8B units | ~−30%+ |
	| 2027E | ~0.7B units | ~−10%+ additional |

	- Forward visibility is poor; supply chain confidence is low

- ## Investment Implications
	- **Long memory/SSD** (Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron): pricing power driven by AI capex supercycle; conventional DRAM supply tightness is a durable tailwind
	- **Cautious on mobile chip vendors** (MediaTek, Qualcomm mobile segment): unit volume contraction + margin pressure from elevated component costs
	- **Cautious on Samsung MX/consumer electronics**: margin collapse happening now; no near-term catalyst for recovery given memory cost structure
	- Asymmetric hedge idea: workers in consumer electronics exposed to volume risk should consider long memory/SSD positions as a natural hedge against job/compensation risk
	- Q3 2026 is the key watch point for whether AI-driven DRAM demand softens or sustains

- ## Open Questions
	- Will premium handset ASPs hold, or does demand elasticity eventually bite even the high end?
	- Can MediaTek/Qualcomm cut costs fast enough to protect margins, or will this show up as earnings misses?
	- Does the DDR4 flatness in late March signal a broader plateauing effect that eventually bleeds into DDR5?
	- Is the 0.8B → 0.7B handset decline baked into current sell-side estimates for MTK/QCOM?
