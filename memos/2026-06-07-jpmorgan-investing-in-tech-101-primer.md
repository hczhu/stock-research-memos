- ## J.P. Morgan — "Investing in Tech 101" (primer)
	- **Source**: J.P. Morgan Asia Pacific Equity Research, *Investing in Tech 101*, Jun 2026 (Gokul Hariharan et al.). A framework primer on tech sector structure, semis ecosystem, and how to invest across the three types of tech stocks.
	- **Big picture**: IT is the **largest sector in the S&P 500 at 39%** of the index; **45+% of MSCI Asia-ex-Japan** (heavy TW/KR weighting). Made of 3 subsectors. **GenAI is the current disruption driver — shifting value toward semiconductors from software vendors** at this stage of the cycle.

- ## The Three IT Subsectors (% = S&P 500 weight)
	| Subsector | S&P 500 weight | Character | Key names |
	|---|---|---|---|
	| **Semiconductors** | **19% of index** | Foundational layer (every device runs on chips); demand indexed to hottest product (AI servers now); cyclical, capital-heavy; geopolitically sensitive (US-China) | NVIDIA, TSMC, ASML, Intel, Broadcom, AMD, SK Hynix |
	| **Hardware & Equipment** | **10% of index** | Physical devices, PCs, phones, servers, networking; less capital-intensive (brand/channel/mfg efficiency); **products commoditize, margins compress**; growth tied to device upgrade cycles | Apple, Cisco, Dell, Hon Hai, Xiaomi |
	| **Software & Services** | **8% of index** | OS, enterprise apps, cloud, IT services; **asset-light, high gross margin, recurring SaaS**; historically valued highly for earnings predictability; **now under GenAI disruption concerns** | Microsoft, Oracle, SAP, Palantir, ServiceNow, TCS |
	- Supply chain: **Upstream semis** (raw materials → SPE vendors / foundries → OSAT / chip designers / memory) → **Downstream hardware** (components → EMS/ODM → system makers/OEM → channel → end demand), with **software** (application + infra/system) feeding end demand.

- ## Moore's Law
	- **Same performance at 20–25% lower cost in 2 years, OR 25–30% better performance at same cost.** "By far the largest productivity improvement seen in any industry."
	- Intel analogy: a 1978 NY–Paris flight ($900, 7 hrs) under Moore's-Law-style gains would now cost **~1 cent and take <1 second**.
	- Driven by **process node migration** (e.g. 7nm→5nm) — shrinking transistors to fit more per wafer (not wafer-size increase, which happens only every 12–15 yrs). Wafer fab lead time **~2+ months**.

- ## Tech Investing — Key Concepts & Themes
	- **1. Digitization & disruption lens** — big tech products are iterative digitizations of human functions: Music (tape→CD→iPod→streaming→AI music); Screen (CRT→LCD→curved→spatial computing); Comms (fixed line→mobile→smartphone→**smart glass / AI pendant?**).
	- **2. The S-curve repeats** — position companies/products at the right growth stage; **mispricing is most likely at early & late stages because the market thinks linearly.** Current positioning:
		- **PCs — past the peak** (shipments ~250mn, off the ~360mn 2011 peak).
		- **Smartphones — past the peak** (~1.1bn, off the ~1.4bn mid-2010s peak).
		- **Servers — in growth phase, driven by AI** (~16mn shipments and rising).
		- **AI — strong token growth from GenAI adoption** (China inference token usage curve inflecting steeply toward **~3.9mn tn by 2030E**).
	- **3. No status quo in tech — far higher churn than other industries.** Top-5 TMT market-cap composition turns over **~every 10 years (2–3 new entrants on average)**; makes comebacks very hard:
	| Rank | Global 2025 | 2015 | 2005 | 1995 |
	|---|---|---|---|---|
	| 1 | **NVIDIA** | **Apple** | Microsoft | Microsoft |
	| 2 | Apple | Google | Intel | IBM |
	| 3 | Google | Microsoft | IBM | Intel |
	| 4 | Microsoft | **Facebook** | **Google** | HP |
	| 5 | **Amazon** | **Oracle** | **Cisco** | Hitachi |
	- (Asia Top-5 2025: TSMC, Tencent, Samsung, **SK Hynix**, Alibaba — vs Alibaba/Samsung/Tencent/TSMC/TCS in 2015.)

- ## The Three Types of Tech Stocks
	- **#1 — Riding the product-cycle S-curve** (multi-baggers; boom-bust)
		- Component vendors / brands geared to a product or trend. **Confirm the trend before entering; valuations "don't matter at all, since EPS will always be wrong."** Chasing the trend works; being contrarian usually doesn't. Cycle turns not from expensive valuation but when **a new product/trend/brand disrupts** the current one.
		- Current S-curve positions (low→high): **Optical → NVDA → EV → Smartphones/PC (past peak)**. NVDA riding the GenAI adoption curve; **QCOM rode 5G until 2022** then de-rated (cautionary tale).
	- **#2 — Perfect cyclicals** (supply-demand-driven)
		- Like petchem/metals: **supply-demand imbalance + time-to-add-capacity drives share prices**; product price (DRAM, wafer) highly correlated to the stock. **Thumb rule: buy when companies are losing money.** Includes power semis, memory (DRAM/NAND), raw wafers.
		- **Where we are now**: Memory — **strong upcycle (question is how long it lasts)**; Wafers — turning after a long downturn; Power semis — recovering from a ~3-yr downturn.
		- **SK Hynix** illustrates deep drawdowns / staggering rallies (2016–19 cycle, and a far larger 2023→2026 cycle with diluted EPS spiking on the current AI/memory upcycle).
	- **#3 — Steady growth** (rare in tech)
		- Strong FCF, stable margins, low capex (not always), good dividends. Own a few key technologies, **not over-dependent on one product/end-market** — e.g. **TSMC makes 95% of AI chips but AI is still <30% of its exposure.** The **"shovel sellers to the gold diggers"** → dodge product-cycle volatility. Examples: **Delta** (power for all verticals), **TSMC** (foundry for anyone without a fab), **ASML** (monopoly in advanced lithography).

- ## Valuation & What Drives Tech Stocks
	- **EPS revisions matter most — more than valuation.** Charts show NVDA/SK Hynix/AMD/TSMC share prices tracking 3-yr forward EPS estimates tightly. "EPS revision momentum trumps valuations." Inventory levels and orders-vs-shipments also help.
	- **Primary valuation tool = 12-month forward P/E** (cuts through near-term cycle noise; trailing P/E misleading for cyclicals whose earnings swing peak-to-trough).
	- **P/Book for extreme cyclicals, especially at cycle turns** — at turns P/E fails (companies over/under-earn), so P/B becomes the anchor at cycle bottoms (pair with ROE).
	- **Reverse DCF > straight DCF** — earnings/cashflow volatility makes straight DCF weak; reverse DCF verifies what's priced in.
	- **Obsolescence kills traditional valuation** — trough price-book / trough P/E must be taken with a grain of salt; a DVD or printer mfg line may have $500M book value but ~zero economic value soon. Comebacks in tech are very hard.

- ## Anatomy of a Semi Cycle (6 stages)
	| Stage | State |
	|---|---|
	| **0** | Demand weak, inventory cleared, market cautious, negative growth, low utilization |
	| **1** | Demand picks up, inventory too low, customers rush orders, new demand drivers found, capacity fills, expansion announced, pricing firms |
	| **2** | Demand healthy + over-ordering; capacity short (2-yr build lead time); prices move up significantly; more capacity announced; growth well above trend |
	| **3** | Demand okay but decelerating; inventory high; mild inventory cuts begin; balance/oversupply in spots; new capacity in sight; growth decelerates but usually still positive |
	| **4** | Demand slows, inventory correction, utilization falls (new capacity online), pricing/revenue decline — similar to Stage 0 |
	| → 0 | Cycle repeats |

- ## End Markets & Geopolitics
	- **2025 semis revenue by end application**: Computer **45%**, Communication **29%**, Automotive **11%**, Consumer **7%**, Industry **7%**, Government **1%**. (Auto remains small despite the narrative.)
	- **Geopolitics**: Re-shoring to US/Europe is **"No" — very difficult & expensive** (reshored fabs face much higher operating costs). **China localization moving faster than expected** at mature nodes, but **leading-edge significantly behind**. The globalized specialization: **~80% of chip-design revenue in US; ~90% of chip-design tools (EDA) from US**; most intricate equipment/materials from **Netherlands & Japan**; **Korea = memory; Taiwan = logic foundry; China = intermediate consumer** (electronic assembly). **Cost of breaking this efficient structure is very high — incremental investment may earn no ROIC.**
